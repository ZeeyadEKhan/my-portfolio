Overview

A storytelling toy that replaces the screen

Baloo is a screenless interactive stuffed animal that tells children branching stories, involves them as characters in the narrative, and lets parents queue content through a companion app, all without a screen in sight.

Built for an interaction design course, Baloo emerged from half a semester of research before a single screen was designed. The team explored four problem areas and converged on childhood screen time, a problem with fast growing consequences and no satisfying solution on the market.

Problem

Screens are the easiest babysitter

Children under 5 are spending more time on screens than ever, often because parents hand over a device out of necessity. The problem is not that parents are negligent. The problem is there is no good alternative.

Three initial topics explored before narrowing to technology and health

Technology's negative effects on health chosen as the focus area

User group pivoted from office workers to children under 5: a more complex and urgent problem with no existing solution

Topic of interest: a non-screen solution that keeps the child safe and gives parents the space to take care of their own tasks

Only 32% of parents see screens as harmful, yet 63% of children exceed the AAP recommended daily screen time limit

Area of opportunity: an engaging and calm non-screen experience that keeps children still and parents productive

How Might We

Reduce excessive screen time while keeping children engaged without supervision, giving parents the peace of mind that their child is safe and the freedom to focus on their own tasks?

Research

Three methods, one picture

Online surveys, user interviews, user observations, and cultural probes built a layered picture of how parents actually use screens with their children, and what they feel about it. The research spoke to both the logistics and the guilt.

Two survey sets: one to known contacts, one distributed to Facebook and Reddit parenting groups

Key survey insight: screens are used as a distraction tool, not as enrichment

Cultural Probes

Cultural probes: in the moment data on the parent and child during the screen exchange, captured in real time by parents themselves

Affinity Mapping

Three key insights: screens keep children still, parents need independence, and children genuinely want to do other things

Screen as Control

Parents consistently used screens to keep children still when they needed to focus elsewhere. It was not neglect. It was the only tool that reliably worked.

Parental Guilt

Parents knew screens were not ideal but felt they had no viable alternative. The guilt was real, but the convenience always won.

Children Want More

Children had a genuine appreciation for activities beyond screens. They loved going outside, playing with toys, and being read to. The screen was not the preference, just the path of least resistance.

The HMW statement that focused the entire concept phase

Concept

Three solutions, one winner

Personas grounded the problem in human terms. Competitive research revealed what the market was missing. Ideation produced three distinct directions, and Baloo emerged as the one that could genuinely eliminate the screen rather than just reduce or replace it.

User Archetypes and Personas

Archetype: the parent who knows screens are harmful but has no replacement for the easy solution they provide

Cindy: a 5th grade teacher who understands both the educational value and the health danger of screens for her child

Cindy's journey map: tension between educational value and anxiety about unmonitored content

Andrew and Lucia: two working parents who feel guilty about evening TV but have no better alternative ready

Andrew and Lucia's journey: a full day of screen moments driven by schedule, not intention

Bethany: 5 years old, social, curious, with a short attention span and a genuine love for varied activities beyond screens

Competitive Research

Codi: closest to the vision but failed on connectivity and real child interaction

YouTube: high accessibility and educational value undercut by inappropriate content risks and screen addiction potential

Woobo: strong emotional intimacy but expensive and undermined by voice identification privacy concerns

Snappy concept: transition screen time into the physical world through an AR camera that turns captures into coloring pages

Tori concept: reduce screen time through an AR coloring book that rewards completion with animation

Three directions: eliminate the screen entirely, transition it to the physical world, or reduce it. The team chose elimination.

Design

Building the bear and the app

Baloo required two parallel design tracks: the physical form of the toy itself, and the parent companion app that gives caregivers full control over what their child experiences. Both had to feel trustworthy, simple, and calm.

Core Features

What makes Baloo unique: AI voice recognition for older children, physical limb buttons for toddlers

Three design questions that drove both prototype rounds

Physical Design

Initial concept sketches: shape symbols on each limb as decision buttons, ear loop for accessory attachment

Charging via wireless rocking chair or USB-C through a concealed zip in the back

Parent App

Initial parent app wireframes: story library, character management, and content categories

Parent app story library: Entertain, Educate, and Explore tabs with curated content for each child profile

Stories queue for hours and download for offline use; parents can add their child's own toys as characters

Settings: per-child profiles, age range controls, topic filters, and character customization

Design Decisions

Why Baloo works the way it does

Four decisions defined the character of the product. Each one came directly from something a real user said or did during research or testing.

Why limb buttons instead of a chest panel?

Early sketches placed all decision buttons on the bear's chest for simplicity. Testing showed children reached for and squeezed the bear's arms and legs naturally during interaction. Distributing one decision per limb matched that behavior and made the interaction feel physical and full-body rather than transactional.

Why a parent app instead of a simpler setup?

Research found that parents would not hand a connected device to a child without understanding and controlling what it could say. Codi failed partly because parents felt locked out of the content. The companion app was not a feature addition. It was the primary trust mechanism that made parental adoption possible.

Why age range controls instead of one story format?

The assumption going into testing was that a single story structure could span ages 1 to 5. Two rounds of user testing proved this wrong immediately. A 5 year old needed complexity and length. A 2 year old needed simplicity and repetition. The age range filter in the parent app exists because there is no single right answer for this age group.

Why background music and sound effects?

Sound was added in the high fidelity round as an enhancement, not a core feature. Testing showed it became the most reliable engagement tool across all age groups. Children who lost interest in the narrative stayed focused when ambient audio was layered in. What started as polish became structural.

User Testing

Two rounds, one refined form

Two rounds of prototype testing across different age groups revealed a fundamental challenge: the same product could not serve a 2 year old and a 5 year old equally well. Each round forced a targeted response and ultimately shaped both the physical form and the story architecture.

Low Fidelity Testing

Low fidelity testing with an interactive PDF: storyline flow, complexity, and attention span assessed across all age groups

Round 1 feedback: 5 year olds engaged strongly; younger children found the complexity too high; character voices kept everyone focused regardless of age

Key Insight

The character voices and narrative kept all children focused regardless of age. Voice was the most powerful engagement tool in the experience, more powerful than story complexity.

Low Fidelity Round 2: Ages 2 to 4

Round 2 focused exclusively on 2 to 4 year olds with a simplified branching structure

Simplified too far: activities were too easy for older children in the range, creating the inverse calibration problem

Potential Storylines

Multiple branching storylines designed to span the 2 to 4 year old range with appropriate complexity at each level

Mid Fidelity and Physical Form

Mid fidelity: farm animal sound matching for younger children; 3 to 4 year olds found it too easy, creating the inverse problem at the other end of the range

Finalized physical form: one decision per limb distributes the interaction naturally across the entire bear body

High Fidelity

High fidelity: sound effects and background music significantly improved engagement and attention span across all age groups

Age Calibration

A single story complexity cannot span ages 1 to 5. The parent app's age range filter and topic controls became essential features as a direct result of testing across all rounds.

Voice Is the Hook

Across every round, character voices kept children focused even when the content was too hard or too easy. Audio engagement was more powerful than story complexity in holding attention.

Physical Interaction

Placing decision buttons on separate limbs rather than the chest or a single limb made the interaction feel natural and full-body. Children reached for the bear instead of just touching a screen.

Sound Effects

Adding background music and ambient sounds to the high fidelity prototype held attention longer and more reliably than narrative alone. Audio layering became a core design feature.

Outcome

A bear that earns the parent's trust

Baloo launched as a fully tested concept across two prototype rounds and multiple age groups. In final testing sessions, children maintained focus for the full story duration without requesting a screen. Parents who reviewed the companion app reported feeling in control of the content for the first time across any storytelling product they had evaluated. The physical form, the parent app, and the story architecture all changed shape at least once as a direct result of something a real user said.

Finalized form: distinct pastel colored limbs with shape symbols make each decision button visually clear for young children

The final Baloo bear, fully realized

Key Finding

Parents do not want to give children screens. They do it because there is nothing better available. Baloo resolves that by providing something genuinely better: a self-directed, safe, and engaging experience that keeps children still without a screen in sight.

What I Learned as a UX Researcher

Leading the research on Baloo reinforced that the most important design decisions happen before any sketch is drawn. The pivot from office workers to children under 5, the discovery that parents use screens out of necessity rather than preference, and the affinity map finding that children genuinely want alternatives to screens: each of these came from rigorous primary research, not assumptions. The product would not have been Baloo without those findings driving every concept decision.

Research Before Concept

Half the course was spent in research before the concept was defined. That investment paid off: the product was grounded in real human behavior, not assumptions about what parents or children want.

Mixed Methods

Surveys, interviews, observations, and cultural probes each revealed something the others missed. No single method would have been sufficient. The full picture only emerged when all four were synthesized together.

Designing for Two Users

Baloo has two users: the child who plays and the parent who controls. Every feature had to satisfy both simultaneously. The parent app was not an afterthought; it was half the product.

Age Is Not One Thing

Designing for ages 1 to 5 means designing for five completely different cognitive stages. Testing exposed this immediately and reshaped the entire product architecture around age range controls and tiered story complexity.
